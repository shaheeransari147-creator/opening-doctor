## Overview

The King's Indian Defense, typically reached via 1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7
(or 3.Nf3 Bg7 4.g3/4.Nc3), is the ultimate hypermodern opening: Black allows
White to build a full classical pawn center with d4, e4, and often c4,
deliberately provoking it, and then attacks that center from a distance with
pieces before undermining it with well-timed pawn breaks, most famously
...e5 or ...c5. Few openings produce such consistently sharp, double-edged
middlegames, which is why the King's Indian has been a favorite weapon of
attacking players from Bronstein and Fischer to Kasparov and beyond.

## Key Ideas and Strategic Plans

In the main line **Classical Variation** (1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6
5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5), the position typically reaches a **Mar del Plata**
structure after 7.O-O Nc6 8.d5 Ne7, where the strategic battle is a pure race
on opposite wings: Black storms the kingside with ...f5-f4, ...g5-g4, and a
rook lift via f6-g6/h6, aiming to mate White's king, while White expands on
the queenside with c5, Nb5/Na4-b6 or b4-c5-c6, aiming to create decisive
threats against Black's queenside/center before the kingside attack lands.
Understanding this race -- whose attack is faster, and which useful "waiting"
or prophylactic moves matter -- is the central skill of King's Indian
players.

In the **Sämisch Variation** (5.f3, preparing Be3/Qd2/O-O-O), White plays
more solidly before committing the king, and Black often responds with
...c5 or ...e5 breaks combined with rapid queenside or central counterplay
rather than the pure kingside pawn storm of the Classical.

## Typical Pawn Structures

The single most important structure to know is the **Mar del Plata / Benoni-
like locked center**: pawns on d5 (White) and d6/e5 (Black) after ...e5 d5,
with the position closed enough in the center that both sides can commit
fully to their respective wing attacks without the center suddenly opening
and punishing whoever is slower. A second key structure arises from the
**Four Pawns Attack** or **Sämisch** setups, where White's broader pawn
center (c4-d4-e4-f4 in the Four Pawns) is more exposed and Black's central
breaks (...c5, ...e5, ...f5) are directly aimed at demonstrating that such an
extended center is overextended rather than strong.

## Common Mistakes

The most damaging King's Indian mistake, especially for Black, is
**mistiming the kingside pawn storm**: pushing ...f5 or ...g4 before
completing preparatory moves (like ...Nc6-e7-repositioning, ...Kh8, or the
rook lift) simply loses time in a race where tempo is everything. Another
common error is **misjudging when to close or keep tension in the center**:
allowing White an easy central break (like c5 or a timely exd5/dxe5 opening
lines) while Black's attack is not yet ready can be fatal, since an open
center favors White's development lead. For White, a frequent mistake is
**playing too slowly on the queenside**, allowing Black's kingside attack to
build unopposed, or **misplacing the king** by delaying O-O-O in Sämisch
lines until Black's central/queenside counterplay is already dangerous.

## Opening Traps

In the Sämisch Variation, an unprepared Black player commonly misplays the
move order around ...a6/...Rb8/...b5 queenside expansion and allows White's
Be3-Qd2-Bh6 plan to trade off the crucial fianchetto bishop, after which
Black's kingside attacking chances evaporate and the king becomes weak
instead. In Classical Mar del Plata lines, a known trap is White playing an
overly hasty Nb5 without adequate preparation, allowing Black's ...a6 and
...axb5 or a timely ...Bxb5-style tactic to win material or seize the
initiative outright.

## Model Game

Fischer's King's Indian games (as Black) are the classical model for the
kingside storm: 1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O
Nc6 8.d5 Ne7 9.Ne1 Nd7 10.Nd3 f5, launching the archetypal ...f5-f4-g5-g4
kingside pawn storm that defines the entire Mar del Plata battle plan.

## Overview

The Queen's Gambit, 1.d4 d5 2.c4, is not a true gambit -- if Black captures
with 2...dxc4 (the Queen's Gambit Accepted), White can always regain the
pawn, most simply with e4 or Qa4+/Bxc4 tricks, because Black's extra pawn on
c4 cannot be defended long-term. The point of 2.c4 is instead to challenge
Black's d5-pawn and open lines, leading to three major branches: the
**Queen's Gambit Declined** (2...e6), the **Slav Defense** (2...c6), and the
**Queen's Gambit Accepted** (2...dxc4). Each produces a distinctly different
type of middlegame, making the Queen's Gambit family one of the richest
opening complexes in all of chess.

## Key Ideas and Strategic Plans

In the **Queen's Gambit Declined**, White's central plan is to build a strong
center with Nc3, Nf3, Bg5 (or Bf4), e3, and eventually to choose between the
classical minority attack (b4-b5 against Black's queenside pawns after an
Exchange Variation, 4.cxd5 exd5) or central expansion with e3-e4 in
Tarrasch-style structures (3...c5). Black's main plans are to solve the
light-squared bishop (often via ...b6/...Bb7 or ...dxc4 followed by ...b5,
the "Vienna"/Tartakower approach), and to generate queenside counterplay
against White's minority attack with ...a5, ...Rb8, and timely piece
activity.

In the **Slav Defense**, Black keeps the light-squared bishop free by
developing it to f5 or g4 before playing ...e6, similar in spirit to the
Caro-Kann's key idea, while contesting c4 directly; main lines revolve around
whether Black holds the extra c4-pawn (Slav Accepted-style lines) or returns
it for quick development and central play.

In the **Queen's Gambit Accepted**, Black returns the pawn but gains free
piece development and, in many lines, aims for quick queenside expansion
with ...c5 and ...b5, fighting for full central and queenside space rather
than accepting White's classical center unchallenged.

## Typical Pawn Structures

The signature Queen's Gambit Declined Exchange structure has symmetric pawns
on the d-file with White pursuing the **minority attack** (b4-b5 to create a
weakness on c6 or an open c-file), one of the most important structural plans
in all of chess to understand: White voluntarily creates a pawn minority on
the queenside specifically because trading it off creates a long-term
weakness in Black's camp (an isolated or backward pawn) that outlasts the
resulting piece simplifications. In Tarrasch-style lines (3...c5), Black
often ends up with an IQP (isolated queen's pawn on d5), trading structural
soundness for piece activity -- directly analogous to Caro-Kann Panov
structures.

## Common Mistakes

A very common mistake in the Queen's Gambit Declined is **misplaying the
minority attack** from either side -- White advancing b4-b5 without adequate
preparation (piece support, king safety) allows Black simple counterplay,
while Black often **fails to generate active piece play** to compensate for
the eventual structural weakness, passively waiting for the weakness to be
exploited instead of racing on the kingside or center. In the Slav, a classic
error is **misordering development**, playing ...e6 before the bishop escapes
to f5/g4, recreating the exact "bad bishop" problem the Slav move order was
designed to avoid. Across all lines, players commonly **release central
tension too early** (an unforced ...dxc4 or cxd5 trade) before finishing
development, handing the opponent an easy time completing their own setup.

## Opening Traps

In the QGA, a classic trap is 1.d4 d5 2.c4 dxc4 3.Nf3 Nf6 4.e3 e6 5.Bxc4 c5
6.O-O a6 7.dxc5! Bxc5 8.Qxd8+ Kxd8, when a careless Black player who forgets
about the queen trade can misplace pieces and lose castling rights for no
compensation. In the Slav, the notorious **Geller Gambit** lines (5.a4 Bf5
6.Ne5, or the sharp Botvinnik Variation) punish any inaccurate Black move
order with immediate central breaks and piece sacrifices on d5/e6 that an
underprepared Slav player can easily misjudge.

## Model Game

Capablanca's Queen's Gambit Declined games remain the classical model for the
minority attack: 1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Bg5 Be7 5.e3 O-O 6.Nf3 Nbd7
7.Rc1 c6 8.Bd3 dxc4 9.Bxc4 Nd5 10.Bxe7 Qxe7 11.O-O Nxc3 12.Rxc3, followed by
b4-b5, showing the textbook minority-attack plan converting a tiny structural
edge into a full point over many patient moves.

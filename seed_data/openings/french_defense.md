## Overview

The French Defense, 1.e4 e6, is a resilient, structurally sound opening built
around the idea of meeting White's central pawn advance with a solid pawn
chain rather than immediate confrontation. After the near-universal 2.d4 d5,
Black offers a central pawn trade and, in most main lines, ends up with a
pawn on d5 fixed against White's e4-pawn once White advances with 3.e5 (the
Advance Variation) or after the tension is resolved in the Winawer (3.Nc3
Bb4) or Classical/Tarrasch (3.Nc3/3.Nd2 Nf6) systems. The defining strategic
feature of almost every French position is Black's **light-squared bishop
problem**: it starts hemmed in behind the e6-d5 pawn chain, and a huge amount
of French theory is about how and when Black solves, trades, or works around
this piece.

## Key Ideas and Strategic Plans

Black's main plans are to challenge White's center with the thematic pawn
breaks **...c5** (attacking d4 from the queenside, the most common French
lever) and, in some lines, **...f6** (attacking e5 once White has advanced
there). The "bad bishop" on c8 is often solved by trading it via ...Bd7-a4/b5,
rerouting it via ...b6/...Ba6 to challenge White's f1-bishop, or simply
accepting it as a defensive piece while Black plays actively on the queenside
with ...c5, ...Nc6, and rook lifts along the c-file.

White's plans depend heavily on the variation: in the **Advance Variation**
(3.e5), White gains space and often targets Black's queenside pawn chain
(d4-e5 wedge) while Black counters on the same wing with ...c5 and piece
pressure on d4; in the **Winawer** (3.Nc3 Bb4), White frequently accepts
doubled pawns after 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 in exchange for the bishop pair
and attacking chances on the kingside, while Black targets the weakened
queenside pawns; in the **Tarrasch** (3.Nd2), White avoids the pin on c3 at
the cost of a slightly passive knight, aiming for a slower buildup.

## Typical Pawn Structures

The archetypal French structure is the **locked pawn chain**: White pawns on
d4-e5, Black pawns on d5-e6, with both sides' plans built around attacking
the base of the opponent's chain (d4 for Black via ...c5, and after
...c5-c4 or exchanges, the c- and d-files become the main battleground). In
Winawer lines, White's doubled c-pawns (c2, c3) are simultaneously a
long-term weakness and a source of extra central control and open lines for
the bishop pair -- evaluating whether the weakness or the activity matters
more in a given position is a core French Defense skill.

## Common Mistakes

The most common mistake for French players is **failing to challenge the
center at all**, playing only kingside development moves (...Be7, ...O-O,
...Nf6) without ever committing to ...c5 or ...f6, which allows White's space
advantage to become overwhelming with no counterplay in sight. Another very
common error is **misjudging the light-squared bishop**: trading it off for
no reason very early is sometimes fine, but shutting it in permanently behind
an unbroken pawn chain while never finding it an active role is a slow-motion
strategic mistake seen constantly at the club level. Players also frequently
**delay castling** while trying to solve the bishop problem with ...Bd7-a4,
leaving the king in the center too long, and **push queenside pawns (...b5)
prematurely**, before ...c5 has even been played, weakening squares with no
concrete follow-up.

## Opening Traps

In the **Advance Variation**, a classic trap runs 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5
4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.Be2?! (instead of the correct 6.a3) cxd4 7.cxd4 Nxd4!
8.Nxd4 Qxd4 winning a clean pawn, punishing White for ignoring the pressure
on d4. In the **Winawer Poisoned Pawn** line (7...Qxa2 lines after 4.e5 c5
5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Ne7 7.Qg4 Qc7 8.Qxg7), both sides must know deep theory --
an unprepared White player very often blunders material trying to punish
Black's pawn grab, while an unprepared Black player just as often walks into
a forced mating attack.

## Model Game

Botvinnik's French Defense games remain the classical model for the
Winawer: 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Ne7 7.a4 Nbc6
8.Nf3 Qa5 9.Qd2 Ba6, showing Black systematically undermining White's
queenside pawn weaknesses while White seeks kingside play with Bd3, O-O and
f4-f5 -- the defining strategic race of the entire variation.
